Built From the Ground Up

The athletes who reach their goals aren't always the most talented — they're the ones who keep showing up when it would be easier not to. Erik Hurley built his coaching philosophy around that truth, the same way he built his own athletic career: one honest session at a time.

A USA Triathlon Certified Coach with a Bachelor of Science in Health Sciences and Sports Medicine, Erik brings academic grounding in exercise science alongside genuine competitive experience to every athlete he works with.

Before he found his home in endurance sport, Erik competed across a range of athletic backgrounds — baseball, basketball, and swimming and diving — developing the all-around movement base and competitive instincts that still inform how he coaches today. Endurance running came next, building from 5K races up through the marathon distance.

Erik has been a triathlete since 2010, racing from sprint distance through the full Ironman. In 2021 and 2022 he brought that competitive experience to a Pacific Northwest triathlon club, providing open-water technique guidance and structured swim programming alongside his own training — hands-on coaching that, combined with his racing background across every triathlon distance, shapes how he builds training for the athletes he works with.

"The only results that last are the ones you earned. Training should be hard enough to drive adaptation, intelligent enough to prevent injury, and honest enough to build real, lasting confidence."

The Approach

How I Coach

Before a single workout gets written, there's one question that matters most: why does this goal matter to you? Not the race on the calendar — the reason underneath it. That answer becomes the anchor for everything that follows: the plan, the hard conversations, and the days when motivation is gone and the work still has to get done.

From there, every plan is built on a principle that doesn't change: consistency is what drives improvement, and consistency depends on a body that isn't constantly breaking down. That belief shapes every decision about training load, progression, and recovery. The goal is never to do more for its own sake — it's to do exactly enough to create adaptation without accumulating a debt the body can't repay.

Progress shows up in efficiency long before it shows up on a results sheet — cadence, sustainable power, the ability to hold the same effort for longer. Those are the signals we build toward together, session by session, until race day simply confirms the work that was already done.
